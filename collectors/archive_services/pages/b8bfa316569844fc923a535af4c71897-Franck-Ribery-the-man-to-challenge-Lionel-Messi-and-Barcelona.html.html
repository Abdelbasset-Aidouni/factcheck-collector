Franck Ribery the man to challenge Lionel Messi and Barcelona

It is surely beyond even the most partisan Premier League enthusiast to deny that Lionel Messi is the best player in the world.

 

By Duncan White
Published: 6:48PM BST 04 Apr 2009

Danger man: Franck Ribery has the talent to unsettle Barcelona's all-stars Photo: AP

What might prove harder to swallow is that English football cannot even lay claim to the next in line: that honour must go to Franck Ribery.

English football may have again dominated the Champions League this season but the best two players in European football will be doing extraordinary things with a football in the one tie that has no direct English interest: Wednesday night's match between Bayern Munich versus Barcelona. Messi versus Ribery will make thrilling viewing.

From the Premier League, only Steven Gerrard, riding the surf of superb form, comes close to matching the individual brilliance of this pair. With Cristiano Ronaldo only sporadically echoing last season's superlative performances, Messi has emerged as the most devastating individual with a ball at his feet and the goal in his sights.

The fact that he has already scored 30 goals in 39 games for Pep Guardiola's Barcelona this season does not adequately convey with what on-field finery those statistics are embellished.

Squat and mop-haired, he weaves his way through defences with impunity and it is the increasingly rare facility to dribble round a succession of opponents that makes him so compelling. Bayern's legendary Franz Beckenbauer believes he has the once in a generation ability of a Maradona or a Pele.

While Messi's exceptional gifts are complemented by the likes of Samuel Eto'o, Thierry Henry and Xavi, Ribery lacks that refined support team at Bayern.

Opponents in the Bundesliga pile on the markers but only injury seems to stop the French winger from tearing teams apart. When he has been fit he has been outstanding, scoring 10 goals in 26 games for his club. Having just returned to fitness, Ribery scored in both of France's back-to-back 1-0 victories over Lithuania last week.

Henry played in those games and has witnessed first-hand Messi's remarkable performances for Barcelona this term. "Messi is Messi and Ribery is Ribery. Nevertheless they do have something in common: they surprise you when you watch them play," Henry said. "They are capable of doing incredible things with the ball in small spaces. When they play it is more about instinct than about tactics."

Henry is right that there is something fundamentally uncoached about both these players. There is one story told about Messi that he does not even bother finding out the identity of his opposite number before a game, just jogs on and plays.

His sinuous style of play is a product of his low centre of gravity, something that may have been affected by the growth-hormone deficiency from which he suffered as a child. He was diagnosed at 11 and he needed extensive and expensive treatment to get him through his adolescent development – and £500 a month was too much for a family from a modest background in Rosario.

Without the treatment we would never have heard of Messi. Newell's Old Boys helped out but when they decided they could not afford it, Barcelona stepped in. He was 13 then and within three years he was in the first team.

Ribery's development was also fraught. As a two year-old he survived a car crash that left him with hundreds of stitches in his face. He remains heavily scarred. However it was his rough upbringing on the sprawling Chemin Vert estate in the industrial fishing city of Boulogne that left a more damaging mark.

He got kicked out of Lille's academy at 16 for failing to do the required schoolwork – a relief to his colleagues who had found his aggression and intensity tiring – and he shuffled between small-scale clubs before moving to Metz. From there he surprisingly joined Galatasaray and after a short, impressive spell in Turkey he moved to Marseille.

Ribery spent much of his youth hanging out with Chemin Vert's North African community and in February 2006 he converted to Islam, encouraged by his wife Wahiba, herself of Algerian origin. His Islamic name is Bilal Yusuf Mohammed, and while he is fiercely private about his faith, he apparently prays before every game.

Bayern paid €25 million for Ribery in the summer of 2007, breaking their club record. Jurgen Klinsmann, who took over last summer, has not had an easy ride this season – the fans have not taken him to heart and the team have underperformed in the league.

He needs trophies to win their conviction. And to win trophies he will need Ribery. Barcelona might be the form team in Europe, and Messi might be the best player in world football, but with Ribery around Barcelona's attacking full-backs, the Brazilians Dani Alves and Sylvinho, will be in for twitchy evening on Wednesday. No wonder Bayern are prepared for a "brutal fight" – general manager Uli Hoeness' words – to fend off the Premier League predators this summer.
